What is a Penny Stock? According to the SEC:

< $5

per share

This simple definition hides a world of complexity and risk. These are shares in small, often developing or distressed companies, not typically found on major exchanges like the NYSE or Nasdaq.

The Allure vs. The Reality

Investors are drawn by the dream of massive returns from a small investment. However, the landscape is dominated by significant, often company-ending risks. This chart shows the conceptual weight of risks versus the potential attractions.

Key Dangers: A Closer Look

Extreme Volatility

Prices can swing wildly on very little news or trading volume. What goes up 100% in a day can just as easily fall to zero.

Crippling Illiquidity

"Thinly traded" means few buyers exist. You might own a stock that's "up," but be completely unable to sell it and realize your profit.

The Hidden Cost: Bid-Ask Spread

You Buy (Ask Price) You Sell (Bid Price)
$0.10 $0.15

This 50% spread means the stock must rise 50% just for you to break even on a sale.

Lack of Information

Many penny stock companies are not required to file financial reports with the SEC, making it nearly impossible to verify their claims or true value.

Where They Trade: The OTC Markets

Most penny stocks trade "Over-the-Counter" (OTC), not on major exchanges. The OTC Markets Group separates companies into tiers based on the information they provide, which acts as a general guide to their risk level.

OTCQX

Best Market

Companies meet high financial standards and are current with their disclosures. Penny stocks are generally excluded from this tier.

OTCQB

Venture Market

For developing companies that must be current in their reporting and undergo annual verification. Can include penny stocks.

Pink Sheets

Open Market

The riskiest tier with no minimum financial standards. This is where most "true" penny stocks with limited or no public information are found.
